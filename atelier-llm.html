Module 1

So what is an LLM, really?

LLM = Large Language Model. At its core, it's a giant mathematical function that takes some text and answers one single question, over and over:

"Given the text so far… what is the most likely next word?"

That's it. It sounds like little, but if you repeat that question millions of times, word after word, you get poems, code, translations and explanations. An LLM doesn't "know" things the way a human does — it has learned statistical patterns from enormous amounts of text.

🧠

What it's made of

Of artificial neurons arranged in layers (a neural network), plus an invention called attention (the transformer). We'll build every piece.

📚

How it learns

It reads billions of sentences, tries to guess the next word, gets it wrong, measures the error and corrects itself a little. Millions of times.

⚙️

What it's used for

Chatbots, coding assistants, translation, summaries, search, agents that use tools — anything involving language.

Step 1 — Tokenization (theory)

A token is the smallest unit the model "swallows". They aren't always whole words: long or rare words get split into sub-words (pieces). Why like this?

  • Finite vocabulary: a model has a fixed list of ~30,000–100,000 tokens. Not every word in the world fits — but from pieces you can rebuild any word.
  • Unknown words: never seen "trainability"? No problem — it's built from "train" + "##abil" + "##ity", known pieces.
  • Efficiency: very frequent words ("the", "of", "and") become a single token; rare ones get split. That way text uses as few numbers as possible.

The ## prefix below means "this piece continues the previous word". Each token is then assigned an ID = its position in the vocabulary.

Module 5 · interactive

Step 2: from ID to meaning — embeddings

An ID (e.g. "cat" = 312) is just a label — it says nothing about meaning. For "312" it's no closer to "dog" than to "airplane". The fix: each token gets a vector — a list of numbers — called an embedding.

What an embedding is (theory)

  • A vector = coordinates. Just as a point on a map has (latitude, longitude), a word has a vector of many numbers (in real models: 768, 4096 or more "dimensions").
  • Meaning = position. The model places words so that those with similar meaning end up close to each other, and different ones — far apart. "cat" and "dog" sit nearby; "cat" and "algorithm" — far.
  • Nobody writes them by hand. The model learns these positions on its own during training: words that appear in similar contexts end up in similar regions. (See Module 8 — training.)
  • Directions encode concepts. That's what enables meaning arithmetic: king − man + woman ≈ queen. The "direction from man to woman" is the same everywhere in the space.

Module 6 · interactive

Attention: the heart of the transformer

To understand a sentence, each word "looks at" the others and decides which ones matter to pay attention to. In "the cat ate quickly because it was hungry", the word "it" must look at "cat". Hover over a word and see what it attends to (the brighter, the more attention):

This is the mechanism that made LLMs possible: instead of reading strictly in order, the model links words located anywhere in the text.

💡 A real model does this across dozens of "attention heads" in parallel, each catching a different kind of relationship (grammar, subject, tone…).

How it learns: rolling down the hill of error

"Training" means: you measure how badly the model is wrong (a number called the loss / error) and change the weights a little in the direction that lowers the error. Repeat. This is gradient descent — like a ball rolling downhill, toward the lowest point.

The ball = the current weight setting. Height = the error. We want to get as low as possible.

The big experiment 🏎️

A neural network teaches itself a track

We put everything we've learned to work. Each car has "eyes" (sensors that measure the distance to the edges of the track) wired to a neural network — exactly the kind of neurons from Modules 2-3. The network decides the steering and the throttle. At first they have no idea how to drive and crash immediately.

But we send out a whole population of cars. The ones that get further are "the best". We take their brains, combine them and mutate them a little at random (like in evolution), and start a new generation. Generation after generation, the cars learn from their mistakes. Watch:

1. The sensors (inputs)

5 rays shoot out from the front of the car. Each returns how far the wall is. These are the 5 input numbers into the network — just like x₁, x₂ from Module 2.

2. The brain (the network)

The 5 numbers pass through layers of neurons and out come 2 decisions: steering (left/right) and throttle. That's what you see animated in the "brain" panel.

3. Selection (evolution)

Whoever goes further without crashing → higher fitness → more likely to become a "parent" for the next generation.

4. Mutation (useful mistakes)

Children inherit their parents' brain + small random changes. Some hurt, some help. The ones that help survive. That's how progress appears.

💡 The link to LLMs: here "learning from mistakes" is visible to the naked eye. An LLM does the same fundamental thing — it tries, measures the error, adjusts — only on text and with gradient descent (Module 8) instead of evolution. The key idea is identical: nobody programs the solution; the model discovers it from feedback.
